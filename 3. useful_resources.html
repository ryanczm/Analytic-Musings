Introduction

This is a list of useful resources I've gathered for self-learning, and I wanted to make a central reference for myself. Unfortunately, my degree does not cover much of the fields I’d like to learn, and thus the onus is to figure out, how best to self-learn these topics. So, I set out to find resources online that I could use, related to finance, data science, and math (in general) and categorized them like so. If you guys have any good suggestions, please tell me! The aim is to cover some of this list, learn it, and apply it in projects to post on this website.

Key Themes

  1. Roadmaps for technical subjects are important, as you need to build up from the basics.
  2. The challenge is to find not just the resources themselves, but a guide that tells you in which order to learn concepts. This is important if the field is in-depth and concepts are built upon a lot of foundational concepts.
  3. Different fields require different ways of thinking, or “metacognition”- being aware of your thought process, so the challenge is to configure your thought process to make it optimal for that particular subject, and be able to “switch” between modes of thought processes.
  4. Doing this will take a lot of time.

Finance

Corporate Finance & Valuation

  1. Investment Banking: Valuation, Leveraged Buyouts, and Mergers and Acquisitions (Joshua Rosenbaum) 2009
  2. Leveraged Buyouts (Paul Pignataro) 2013
  3. Financial Modelling (Simon Benninga) 2014
  4. An Introduction to Investment Banks, Hedge Funds, and Private Equity (David Stowell) 2009
  5. Investment Valuation (Aswath Damodaran) 1995
  6. Best Practices for Equity Research Analysts (James Valentine) 2011
  7. McKinsey: Valuation (Tim Koller) 1990
  8. Financial Modelling in Practice (Michael Rees) 1990

General Markets/Random:

  1. The Alchemy of Finance (George Soros) 1994
  2. A Random Walk Down Wall Street (Burton Malkiel) 1973
  3. The Intelligent Investor (Benjamin Graham) 1949
  4. Stock Market Wizards (Jack Schwager) 1949

Programming:

Python

  1. Corey Schafer's playlist is quite comprehensive for an introduction. There are 143 videos. I've only done like a third so far.
  2. Derek Banas’ YouTube channel for the common visualization libraries and packages (Numpy, Pandas, MPL, SNS, Plotly, etc)
  3. Books: I feel like it’s quite easy to find books on Python, so I didn’t bother searching for any.

R:

  1. Books on R by Garrett Grolemond and Hadley Wickham (Tidyverse creator) and Robert Kabacoff. I feel like books are better to learn for R, for some reason.

DBMS/SQL:

  1. Stanford's Jennifer Widom's DBMS/SQL courses on EDX were highly recommended.
  2. The Worlds of Database Systems: the book Widom wrote.

Since there are so many textbooks and resources on maths, I found this roadmap which tells you in which order to start learning and what books to find. I figured it would be better to follow a roadmap rather than just list random courses and books. If anyone has any idea how to approach this, let me know. Mathematics is probably the hardest subject to achieve a decent proficiency with self-learning. When I start on this in the near future, I'll do some research and update this section with more subject-specific details accordingly.

I think the issue is more towards a trade-off: how much time to spend or "give up" learning, and the payoff received for doing so; perhaps being more cognizant, understanding ML better, etc.
