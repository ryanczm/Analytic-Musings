A First Foray into Quant Analysis: An Elementary Pairs Trading Strategy Implementation & Evaluation on NDX Equities

23 March 2023 · 15 min read   [ Quantitative Finance ]

Strategy Overview

Introduction

For those of you who have actual experience as a QR or QT, this post might seem painfully amateurish. And that’s because it is. In this post I implement a simple pairs trading strategy based on bivariate cointegration tests in the selection phase on NASDAQ 100 equities. The selection phase will be based on data from 2018-2021. The trading & evaluation phase will be from 2021-2023. I then analyze further trends between cointegration, returns and sector in the data, and provide possible solutions to overcome the problem of cointegration tests having low power. The repo is here. No third party backtesting or analysis libraries were used.

Caveats and Assumptions

Certain implementation aspects were not addressed that would make such a strategy impractical live:

  • Survivorship Bias – The in-sample period starts from 2018 so I should have simulated the strategy using the historical index constituents of the NASDAQ 100 in 2018, but finding these was surprisingly non-trivial.
  • T-costs, Liquidity & Market Impact – Wide spreads mean higher t-costs for more frequent trades (in addition to commission), and low book depth means slippage for larger trade sizes.
  • Spurious/Decaying Cointegration – Cointegration could be spurious or decay quickly.
  • In-sample Cointegration does not predict Out-Sample Cointegration – The main problem, sort of a weak-form EMH for cointegration, which I find out later in this post, and offer some possible solutions to overcome this problem.

Selection & Trading Phase

Universe & Cointegration Tests

The data used was daily OHLCV from yfinance. I didn’t adjust for corporate actions (share buybacks, dividend payouts). I then select pairs based off the bivariate Engle-Granger cointegration test on the in-sample period (2018-2021) From my understanding, this test first regresses one series against the other to get the beta coefficient, creates the spread/residual series from the linear combination of the two, then performs a unit root test on the spread:

This lets us select pairs which are cointegrated for the in-sample period (2018 to Jan 2021). However, more pairs and tests exposes us to multiple comparison bias, which invalides the interpretation of p-values as the number of tests increase. To adjust this, we can use the Bonferroni correction, which adjusts our statistically significant p-value cutoff. I didn't do that here.

Trading Phase

Once pairs are selected, we compute a spread for each pair. Then, we smooth it out, and set entry/exit thresholds. The spread is computed off a dynamic hedge ratio, evolved in online, recursive fashion via a Kalman filter (which turns out to be surprisingly effective at keeping a spread stationary even if the underlying assets are not as cointegrated!)

The spread is then z-scored via two rolling windows. The shorter average of the past $z_s$ days is demeaned by a longer average of the past $z_l$ days, then divided by the standard deviation of $z_l$. We enter positions when this spread is above $[z,-z]$ and exit when it crosses 0. If it crosses the top, long the bottom asset and short the top asset to form the top leg, and vice versa. The parameters are thus the short (5D) and long (30D) windows and the thresholds (0.5 and -0.5).

Performance

Before analyzing the relationship between sectors, cointegration and returns more closely, I first examine the out-of-sample performance (start 2021 to 2023 Feb) of selected sets of pairs.

Sharpe Ratio Caveats

One problem I had with calculation of returns and Sharpe was the initial capital for a pair. If we start from 0 initial capital, the first few months tends to give very volatile returns. Returns for the first few round trip trades tend to be disproportionate, despite portfolio value changing very little, as seen by the equity curve. This increases volatility and compresses the Sharpe ratio calculation.

I decided to smooth it out by starting with initial capital equal to the hedge ratio times the stock price at the start of the period. I’m not sure if this is corrrect.

Sector vs No Sector Restriction Performance

I then iterated through all selected pairs with a p-value less than the arbitary cutoff of 0.10: ~430 pairs with no sector restriction and ~96 pairs with sector restriction.

The average mean and median annualized Sharpe for both sets of pairs was roughly the same ~0.60, and ~0.82. However, from the histogram, it is evident the no-sector restriction pairs has an extreme left tail of poor performers while the same-sector pairs had a floor on bad performance. This would support the hypothesis that cointegrated same-sector pairs are less likely to perform poorly out-sample because they have a higher likelihood of some underlying economic relationship.

IR was decent with a median of ~0.35 and a mean of ~0.45, beating the benchmark. However, I am not sure if IR is relevant here because pairs trading is market neutral anyway. We can then plot cumulative (mean) returns against the benchmark:

Cointegration, Returns & Sector Analysis

I wanted to analyze the relationships between sector, cointegration and return. Note - These analyses are built on the assumption that p-values are continuous and a measure of 'strength of evidence' - in this case, a measure of the strength of cointegration.

There seems to be some debate in statistics whether p-values should be interpreted as binary outcomes (effect or no effect) or as a continuous measure (strength of effect). I don't claim to understand the details, but it seems the debate leans towards that p-values should not (see here) be treated as a continuous variable indicating strength of evidence. If this is the case, then unfortunately, this section is not that useful.

Cointegration as an Alpha Factor

How well does cointegration imply Sharpe? Ideally, we would run the strategy for all pairs (~4000), calculate their in-sample Sharpes, then compute the Spearman rank or Pearson correlation coefficient with p-values. Due to computation, I did this for the top and bottom deciles of p-value (<0.10 and >0.80) as a form of pseudo-quantile analysis.

I would have expected a stronger correlation. However (not shown here) I think the high Sharpe ratios of non-cointegrated pairs seem to be due to the power of the Kalman filter and my very simplistic backtest. When I plotted spreads of least cointegrated pairs, they all looked quite stationary, so I am attributing this to the Kalman filter.

Cointegration Decay & Transfer Coefficient

To what extent does in-sample cointegration imply out-sample cointegration? Unfortunately, there is little to no relationship as demonstrated by the scatterplot:

This implies that cointegration is highly non-stationary, and cointegration is determined by factors/variables that are external to price data itself, similar to the weak form of EMH. Thus, I would try to find external variables that are leading indicators of cointegration, which would help predict future cointegration for a period, rather than rely on tests themselves.

Conclusion

Problems with Predicting Cointegration - Possible Ideas

So unfortunately, it does seem that in-sample cointegration is a very poor predictor of out-sample cointegration. So, my guess would be to try and find other kinds of predictors. For example, if certain indicators for certain stocks predicted future cointegration, then we could decide which assets were likely to be cointegrated in the future and allocate weight towards them. E.g:

  1. Choose predictors based off prior economic hypotheses, say some set of variables available currently (e.g March) to predict next month's cointegration for the next 3 months (e.g April-Jun) for some stocks (e.g one subsector).
  2. Convert p-values to binary outcomes. This avoids the problem of treating p-values as continuous.
  3. Train a classifier to predict lookahead cointegration - e.g a boosting model.
  4. If it works (e.g sufficient predictive power), examine feature importances/coefficients for the variables and flag interesting ones..
  5. Repeat this process for different sets of variables and historical time windows and refine hypotheses.
  6. Use these variables to identify future cointegrated pairs to and trade them, rerunning the model batch-style.

Learning Points

From this exercise, I’ve definitely got some practice to analyzing and handling financial data, and practicing my visualisation skills as well. However, there are many things I should do that I did not manage to do in this post:

  • Simulate and understand the impact of transaction costs and liquidity (commissions, market impact) which is especially important in a small-margins strategy like pairs trading.
  • Simulate running a strategy in a periodic fashion (e.g rerunning the models every quarter) to rebalance the portfolio of pairs trade
  • Aspects of portfolio optimization or weight allocation
  • Simulate risk management measures (e.g exit rule if pair doesn’t close after x weeks).
